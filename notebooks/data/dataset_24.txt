Mushroom Dataset

Tags: mythbusting, OpenML100, study, trivial, uci

Source: UCI - 1981

Please cite: The Audubon Society Field Guide to North American Mushrooms (1981). New York: Alfred A. Knopf

Description:
This dataset describes mushrooms in terms of their physical characteristics. They are classified into two categories: poisonous or edible. The data originates from The Audubon Society Field Guide to North American Mushrooms, published in 1981. It includes descriptions of hypothetical samples corresponding to 23 species of gilled mushrooms in the Agaricus and Lepiota Family. Each species is identified as definitely edible, definitely poisonous, or of unknown edibility and not recommended. The latter class was combined with the poisonous category.

The Guide explicitly states that there is no simple rule for determining the edibility of a mushroom; no rule like "leaflets three, let it be" for Poisonous Oak and Ivy. This underscores the complexity of mushroom classification and the importance of expert knowledge in this domain.

Attributes Information:
The dataset contains numerous attributes describing various physical characteristics of mushrooms. These include cap shape, cap surface, cap color, presence of bruises, odor, gill attachment, gill spacing, gill size, gill color, stalk shape, stalk root, stalk surface above and below the ring, stalk color above and below the ring, veil type, veil color, ring number, ring type, spore print color, population, and habitat.

Each attribute has multiple possible values, represented by single letter codes. For example, cap shapes can be bell, conical, convex, flat, knobbed, or sunken. Cap surfaces may be fibrous, grooved, scaly, or smooth. The dataset provides a comprehensive encoding scheme for all attributes, allowing for detailed mushroom descriptions.

Relevant Papers:
Several research papers have utilized this dataset for various machine learning and data mining studies. These include works on concept acquisition, trading off simplicity and coverage in incremental concept learning, and extraction of logical rules from training data using neural networks.

The mushroom dataset has proven valuable for testing and developing various machine learning algorithms, particularly in the areas of classification and rule extraction. Its rich attribute set and binary classification problem make it an interesting challenge for researchers in the field of artificial intelligence and data science.
